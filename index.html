Research Study

Why Do Some Students Struggle in Mathematics?

Evidence from Secondary Schools in Delta State, Nigeria

Uzor Blessing Ngozi
Mathematics Education Researcher | Data-Driven Education Advocate

Abstract

Mathematics remains a gateway subject for science, technology, and economic participation, yet many Nigerian students continue to perform below expectations. Why do students in the same schools—and often the same classrooms—experience Mathematics so differently? This study explores that question by centering the voices of learners themselves.

Drawing on survey data from 240 senior secondary school students in Ethiope East Local Government Area, Delta State, Nigeria, the research examines how students perceive the influence of parental education, teacher experience, teacher qualifications, socioeconomic background, and cultural-linguistic context on their academic performance.

Findings show that teacher quality and family economic support are perceived as the most influential factors, while cultural background and parental occupation are viewed as far less determinative. These results challenge deficit-based narratives and highlight the need for equity-focused educational policy and teacher development.

Research Context

In Nigeria, poor performance in Mathematics is often explained in simple terms: students are weak, parents are uneducated, or culture is the problem.

But these explanations rarely ask the most important question:

What do students themselves believe is shaping their learning?

This research takes student perception seriously—not as opinion, but as evidence. Students are the ones who experience classrooms daily, navigate family constraints, and interact directly with teachers. Understanding how they interpret these influences offers a grounded, human lens for improving Mathematics education.

Methodological Approach

Design Descriptive survey research
Participants 240 Senior Secondary School Two (SSS2) students
Schools Six public secondary schools
Location Ethiope East LGA, Delta State, Nigeria

Key Findings

Teacher Quality Matters—A Lot

Students consistently identified teacher experience and professional qualification as major influences on their Mathematics performance. Experienced teachers were perceived as better at explaining concepts and managing classrooms.

However, an important nuance emerged: While qualified teachers were seen as more effective, they were not automatically seen as making students love Mathematics.

Family Income Shapes Opportunity

Students strongly associated low family income with difficulties in Mathematics learning. Key issues included inability to afford textbooks and learning materials, limited access to private study support, and stress caused by unmet basic needs.

These findings reinforce the idea that academic performance is not just about ability, but also about access and stability.

Parental Education Helps—but Is Not Everything

Students perceived parental education as beneficial, especially when parents could assist with assignments, monitor school progress, and encourage academic effort.

However, parental education alone was not viewed as determinative. Students from less-educated households did not automatically see themselves as disadvantaged, pointing again to resilience and the role of school-based support.

Culture Is Not the Villain

One of the most important findings of this study is what students rejected. Most students disagreed with the idea that speaking local languages or Pidgin English at home causes poor academic performance.

This challenges long-standing cultural-deficit narratives and suggests that students locate the problem not in their identity or heritage, but in structural and instructional conditions.

Students do not view themselves or their backgrounds as the problem. They point instead to unequal access to resources, differences in teacher preparation, and economic constraints that limit learning opportunities.

Implications for Education Policy and Practice

  • Teacher development programs should prioritize both content mastery and motivational pedagogy, addressing the gap between instructional competence and student engagement.
  • Equity-focused interventions—such as learning material support for low-income students—are critical for addressing socioeconomic barriers to Mathematics learning.
  • Culturally responsive teaching should be encouraged, not as a remediation tool, but as an asset-based approach that values students' linguistic and cultural backgrounds.
  • Policymakers must recognize that improving Mathematics performance requires addressing economic and institutional factors, moving beyond cultural stereotypes to structural solutions.
  • Future research should combine perception data with achievement outcomes and use longitudinal designs to track performance over time and across different regions.
